Extrinsic and intrinsic motivation

Extrinsic motivations are what you get for completing a project, such as money, fame, encouragement, likes, subscribers, or avoiding punishment. They are the rewards you get in exchange for being done.

Intrinsic motivations come from the project itself, and there are four types:

  • Autonomy - You decide what to do
  • Growth - You learn something new, or practice something you're rusty at
  • Excellence - You do a good job
  • Usefulness - The project is valuable to you, your family, or the community

Continue ›

Extrinsic motivations work well for routine, repetitive tasks - things you can do automatically, without a lot of thought or planning.

For example, a dealership auto mechanic spends his day diagnosing and fixing a specific brand of cars. The procedures are well known, require little thought or choice, and the paycheck is his extrinsic reward.

Intrinsic motivations work well for creative tasks.

If that same mechanic goes home to work on a project race car, he has intrinsic motivations:

  • He chooses what to do, and how to do it.
  • He learns about a different class of vehicle
  • He does a good job, and the car looks good
  • He wants to start racing

While working on his a project car, the mechanic gains a deep sense of satisfaction.

He enjoys the process.

Decreasing motivation

Having extrinsic motivations will decrease the intrinsic motivations.

In one study, nursery school students were given markers and encouraged to draw pictures during their free play time, an activity for which they had high intrinsic interest.

Several weeks later, students in one group were given a “good player” award with a ribbon and gold star for drawing, while students in the control group were simply encouraged to draw for fun.

Several weeks after that, both groups were monitored when the markers were brought out during playtime.

Surprisingly, the children who had been given an extrinsic reward showed far less interest in drawing than the children who were not offered the reward.

Something about the reward reduced the children’s desire to draw.

Continue ›

 

In another study, two groups of college students were asked to perform activities that they already found interesting: solving puzzles and writing newspaper headlines.

In the first phase of the study, both groups performed the activity with no rewards.

In the second phase, one group was paid for the activity, while the second group acted as a control.

In the third phase, both groups once again performed the activity with no rewards.

Students who were paid during the second phase were less likely to continue the activities in the third phase. Students who were not paid were more likely to continue.

Both groups started out equally interested, but the extrinsic reward reduced the motivation for one group.

Continue ›

 

This is called the overjustification effect.

Essentially, your mind notices you're being rewarded for an activity, and assumes this is the reason you're doing it. Extrinsic motivations replace the intrinsic ones, and your intrinsic motivations fade away.

But then, the extrinsic rewards only come when the project is complete...

So with no intrinsic motivation to sustain you, making progress on your projects becomes a boring drudgery. You lose interest over time, and eventually give up.

If you have trouble finishing your projects, it's probably because your intrinsic motivations have been replaced with extrinsic ones, leaving you no motivation to sustain you while working on your project.

You'll need to fix that.

The concordant self image

Your actions can change your opinions.

In one study researchers asked students to perform a boring task for an hour. Once the hour was up, the researchers asked half to try to convince another person (an actor) that the task was interesting and engaging.

Curiously, the subjects who tried to convince a third person that the task was actually interesting rated the tasks less boring than the subjects in the control group.

Their brains noticed a disconnect between their beliefs and actions, and changed their opinions to make their self image consistent with their actions.

Continue ›

 

In a second study, when participants were asked to give an impromptu speech supporting an opinion they disagree with, their private opinion moves closer to the opinion in the speech.

Again, it seems that their brains noticed the disconnect between what they believed and what they did, and their brains changed to make a more consistent self image.

We can use this effect to change how your brain sees your project motivations.

The neurochemistry of motivation

Knowing the neurochemistry of motivation can help. Knowing how things work and how they interrelate can help you make changes that will work, instead of making changes based on guesswork.

The following pages describe, in broad terms, how the brain manages motivation using neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors. We start with a quick overview of these concepts.

If you already know about neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors, feel free to skip the overview and begin with the section on motivation.

If you don't know what neurotransmitters are, or you would like a refresher, start with that section: it will lead you to the motivation section after.

Neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors

Neurotransmitters are just chemicals that one neuron uses to signal another neuron.

This signalling happens at a special point called a "synapse" on the neuron body - it's a place where the body of one neuron almost touches the body of another. Whenever one neuron wants to signal another, it releases neurotransmitter molecules at the synapse junction. The molecules diffuse over to the other neuron and bind to the corresponding receptor, sending a signal that excites the target neuron.

It's sort of like the bell at a hotel desk. Sending neurotransmitters through the synapse is like ringing a bell: it gets the attention of the target neuron.

Once the signal is delivered, the receptor releases the neurotransmitters. The molecules diffuse back to the originating neuron, where they are collected and stored for future signaling.

Neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors

There are over 100 neurotransmitters used in the brain, but for our purposes we only need to know about three: NorEpinephrine, Dopamine, and Serotonin, which are responsible for activity, pleasure, and reward.

Continue ›

Before we begin, we need to sort out some nomenclature.

Adrenaline and Epinephrine are two names for the same chemical. It was discovered independently by two laboratories at about the same time. NorEpinephrine and NorAdrenaline are two names for a different chemical. It was discovered by the same laboratories at about the same time.

Epinephrine and NorEpinephrine are the names used in the US, while Adrenaline and NorAdrenaline are used in the UK. Across the rest of the world different countries use one term or the other.

Such is the way of science.

For the rest of the course, this program will use both terms with a slash between the equivalent names. Epinephrine/Adrenaline will be the "fight-or-flight" neurotransmitter, and NorEpinephrine/NorAdrenaline will be the "action" neurotransmitter.

Continue ›

NorEpinephrine/NorAdrenaline, the neurotransmitter of action

Adrenaline/Epinephrine prepares your body (and mind) for a jolt of activity: fight or flight, something you might have to do to survive.

NorAdrenaline/NorEpinephrine is a weaker version that's used for day-to-day decision making. Every time you decide to do something (or not to do something), your brain uses a little bit of Adrenaline/Epinephrine to start the action.

You may have heard the expression "burning the candle at both ends".

If NorAdrenaline/NorEpinephrine is like burning a candle, Adrenaline/Epinephrine is like burning rocket fuel.

Ignore Failure

Thomas Edison had over 10,000 failed attempts before he got a working light bulb.

When asked by a reporter how it felt to fail 10,000 times, Edison replied: “I didn't fail 10,000 times, I’ve just found 10,000 ways that will not work.”

This lesson isn't about getting more motivation now, it's about keeping motivation when you encounter a failure.

Imagining future failures can minimize their impact. Also, thinking about failure can encourage taking steps to avoid it.

Notice we said "when" and not "if". It's a near certainty that you'll encounter failure at some point, so getting properly oriented will help.

The term for this is "prospection".

Continue ›

So then, how should we handle failure?

First, note that failure comes at the end of a project, or perhaps causes the end. If you're still trying, if there's still a way it might work, then you haven't failed.

Next, in almost all cases failure is a stepping stone to success. There are innumerable examples of people failing repeatedly, only to succeed in the end. Thomas Edison is an extreme example (10,000 tries? Yikes!), but the principle happens everywhere.

And finally, even when a project fails, is it a complete failure?

Consider:

  • Did you learn anything new?
  • Did you improve your technique?
  • Can you salvage something useful for another project?

It's extremely rare for a project ro fail completely. In nearly all cases there is still value in having made the journey.

Flow States

A flow state is when you're doing something and you lose track of time. Many people experience flow states while driving - you're thinking about something, turning over a problem in your mind, and arrive at your destination without any memory of driving or of the passage of time.

Some people experience flow states in their projects. They sit down to work, get caught up in the activity, and look up hours later wondering where the time went.

Flow states are enjoyable, and the enjoyment builds the longer you are in the state. When you enter flow, it's as if your brain starts a counter, and the higher the count the more pleasure you get. When you stop working, the counter resets to zero, and if you restart your counter needs to build back up before you can get a similar level enjoyment.

The flow state counter will reset every time you stop working, so for maximum pleasure you can seek to avoid interruptions and breaks while in a flow state.

The Cognitive Gas Tank

One curious result from psychological studies is that cognition is a limited resource.

It's as if you wake up each morning with a gas tank full of cognitive ability, and throughout the day every action you take and every decision you make uses up a little bit of it.

During sleep, your gas tank fills up so that, hopefully, you wake up refreshed, with a full tank.

Continue ›

Studies show that it's possible to drain your cognition completely, and when that happens you lose the ability to make rational decisions.

This explains how you can come home at night and feel exhausted, even though you know you're not physically tired. Your cognitive gas tank is sitting at empty, and all you want to do is veg out in front of the T.V.

"Daddy's too tired to play with you tonight, pumpkin. Let's take a rain check."

Continue ›

It's OK to feel exhausted at the end of the day occasionally - we're probably evolved for that - but if you're feeling exhausted every evening then that should be fixed. Constant mental exhaustion is the same as constant mental stress (it uses the same neurotransmitters), and this causes incremental damage.

It causes depression, reduces your immune function, and leads to all sorts of problems later in life.

It's sort of like smoking. The damage from a single cigarette probably won't affect your health appreciably, but smoking continually over several years and the damage will add up.

Are you often mentally exhausted at the end of the day?

This will greatly reduce your life enjoyment, and you need to fix it.

You need to change in such a way that your lifestyle isn't as demanding. There are no ready answers for how to do this. It might be as simple as learning to say "no" to people around you who demand your time at your job, or in your home.

It might require changing jobs, or changing where you live, or finding new friends, or changing loved ones.

That's not something this program can help with, other than to point to the problem.
