Overview

Many people have difficulty bringing their projects to completion. They get excited at first, but the passion soon fades and the project languishes for lack of time.

Then you get a new idea that seems wonderful and exciting, but in a little while this new excitement also fades, and now you have two projects languishing for lack of time.

Rinse, and repeat.

Over time, many people amass multiple, sometimes dozens, of projects that they recognize as valuable, and would "really like to get back to", but never seem to have the time or motivation to do so.

Continue ›

 

This program uses psychological methods to increase your motivation for completing projects. Run it once a day for 30 days, and you should see an improvement in project motivation.

Each run consists of a one-minute slideshow of words and images that bring your project to mind, plus a short lesson on some aspect of motivation.

The slideshow gets your mind thinking about the project, and the lessons help you make small changes to your behavior and attitude that should boost your project motivation.

The techniques are documented and backed by scientific studies, no tricks or subliminal messaging is used. The techniques are described in the "Methods" link on the main page.

Q: Does this work?
 
A: No one knows.

The premise of this program is that extrinsic goals have replaced your intrinsic motives, and your intrinsic motives have faded. That this happens is well established in the literature.

What isn't known, because it hasn't been studied, is whether this situation can be reversed. Hence this program.

It's 5 minutes of your time each morning for 30 days.

Try it, it's experimental, and if it doesn't work you're no worse off than you were at the start.

Extrinsic and Intrinsic Motivation

Extrinsic motivations are what you get for completing a project, such as money, fame, encouragement, likes, subscribers, or avoiding punishment. They are the rewards you get in exchange for being done.

Intrinsic motivations come from the project itself, and there are four types:

  • Autonomy - You decide what to do
  • Growth - You learn something new, or practice something you re rusty at
  • Excellence - You do a good job
  • Usefulness - The project is valuable to you, your family, or the community

Continue ›

 

Extrinsic motivations work well for routine, repetitive tasks - things you can do automatically, without a lot of thought or planning.
 
For example, a dealership auto mechanic spends his day diagnosing and fixing one brand of cars. The procedures are well known, require little thought or choice, and the paycheck is his extrinsic reward.

Intrinsic motivations work well for creative tasks.
 
If that same mechanic works at home on a project race car, he has intrinsic motivations:

  • He chooses what to do, and how to do it.
  • He learns about a different class of vehicle
  • He does a good job, and the car looks good
  • He wants to start racing

While working on his a project car, the mechanic gains a deep sense of satisfaction.
 
He enjoys the process.

Decreasing motivation

Extrinsic motivations will decrease the intrinsic motivations.

In one study, nursery school students were given markers and encouraged to draw pictures during their free play time, an activity for which they had high intrinsic interest.

Several weeks later, students in one group were given a “good player” award with a ribbon and gold star for drawing, while students in the control group were simply encouraged to draw for fun. Several weeks after that, both groups were monitored when the markers were brought out during playtime.

Surprisingly, children given an extrinsic reward showed far less interest in drawing than the children who were not offered the reward.

Something about the reward reduced the children’s desire to draw.

Continue ›

 

In another study, two groups of college students were asked to perform activities that they already found interesting: solving puzzles and writing newspaper headlines.

In the first phase of the study, both groups performed the activity with no rewards.

In the second phase, one group was paid for the activity, while the second group acted as a control.

In the third phase, both groups once again performed the activity with no rewards.

Students who were paid during the second phase were less likely to continue the activities in the third phase. Students who were not paid were more likely to continue.

Both groups started out equally interested, but the extrinsic reward reduced the motivation for one group.

The Overjustification Effect

This is called the overjustification effect.

Essentially, your mind notices being rewarded for an activity, and assumes this is the reason you're doing it. Extrinsic motivations replace your intrinsic ones, and the intrinsic motivations fade.

But then, the extrinsic rewards only come when the project is complete...

So with no intrinsic motivation to sustain you, making progress on your projects becomes a boring drudgery. You lose interest over time, and eventually give up.

If you have trouble finishing your projects, probably your intrinsic motivations have been replaced with extrinsic ones, leaving no motivation to sustain you while working on your project.

You'll need to fix that.

Actions can change Beliefs

Your actions can change your beliefs.

In one study researchers had two groups perform a boring task for an hour. Subjects from the first group were then asked to convince another person (an actor) that the task was interesting and engaging, while the second group acted as a control.

Curiously, subjects who tried to convince a third person that the task was actually interesting rated the tasks less boring than the subjects in the control group.

Their brains noticed a disconnect between their beliefs and their actions, and changed their beliefs to become consistent.

Continue ›

Concordant self-image

In another study, when participants were asked to give an impromptu speech supporting an opinion they disagreed with, their private opinion moved closer to the opinion in the speech.

Again, it seems that their brains noticed the disconnect between what they believed and how they acted, and changed beliefs to make a more consistent self image.

Your brain tries to maintain a concordant self-image. When your actions are inconsistent with your beliefs, your mind will change beliefs to match your actions.

You can use this effect to change your project motivations.

Mantras

The concordant self-image technique uses actions to change your project beliefs.
 
Masking the rewards portion of your list will help change your beliefs, but this is only one action.


You can get a stronger effect by using more actions.
 
We do this by adding a mantra: a word or phrase recited during the day to reinforce the motivational beliefs.

Mantras get a lot of bad press, and probably for good reason. They are used for all sorts of vague and sketchy reasons, with a strong mystical component.
 
We won't be doing anything mystical, just adding an action to help your self-image become concordant. Concordant with a maker who enjoys the process.

Continue ›

Homework

Look over your motivation list and think of a mantra that will reinforce your self-image. Something that speaks to you, something you like.

Nothing long, just a couple of words. It can be generic, such as "I enjoy the process", or specific to your project such as "I love writing detective novels".

Use personal pronouns and put your verb in present tense. Starting with "I" is typical.
 
A mantra should also be positive, in the logical sense. don't say "I don't" or "I won't", brains have a harder time with inverted logic. Phrase it in terms of what you will do, instead of what you won't.
 
For a stronger effect, write it out.

Continue ›

 

Over the course of the next day as you have spare moments, recite your mantra to yourself. Do this frequently, and try to visualize yourself living up to the intent.
 
This will encourage the concordant self-image process.
 
If you like, along with repeating your mantra you can write it out a couple of times during the day. Writing the mantra has a stronger effect than simply repeating it, but no one knows why this is.

Motivational focus

Intrinsic motivations work well for creative tasks, and extrinsic motivations (rewards) work well for dull, repetitive tasks.

Some projects are a mixture of both.

For example, a car project might end with wet-sanding and painting. Sanding and painting might not be as interesting as the engine rebuild.

An electronics project might be followed by video editing and dubbing sessions for a YouTube channel.

And after creating a beautiful ceramic piece, a tedious session of photographing, writeup, pricing, and listing may be required.

If your project has a boring part at the end, you can change the endpoint of your project.

Place your motivation list on top of an empty sheet of paper, and fold the bottom up over the existing end point. Write in a new end point up to, but not including, the boring bits that come at the end.


As before, tape the flap shut and tell yourself you won't worry about those tasks.

You can also try switching motivations. This can work if a long, boring task comes in the middle. When you reach the boring task, switch from intrinsic motivations to extrinsic ones.

Promise yourself a reward for successful completion: go out to dinner, eat a bowl of ice cream, take a walk in the park - whatever you enjoy, use that as a reward for finishing the boring section.

Keep your task motivations separate. Any overlap between the rewards and the enjoyment will reduce your enjoyment.

Be clear with yourself that the rewards are for completing the task, and not for the entire project. Think about the rest of the project in terms of intrinsic enjoyment.

The neurochemistry of motivation

Knowing the neurochemistry of motivation can help. Knowing how things work and how they interrelate will help you make informed decisions about your motivation.

The next few lessons describe, in broad terms, how the brain manages motivation using neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors.

The scientific literature for neurochemistry has changed since the author first learned it 10 years ago. It's also wildly complex, and there's no consensus.

What follows is believed to be accurate for what happens and can be used for illustration and planning, but the names might be wrong.

Continue ›

Neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors

Neurotransmitters are chemicals that one neuron uses to signal another.

This happens at a special point called a synapse, where one neuron almost touches another. When the neuron wants to send a signal, it releases neurotransmitter molecules at the synapse, which diffuse to the other neuron and bind to corresponding receptors.


It's sort of like ringing a desk bell. Sending neurotransmitter molecules through the synapse alerts the target neuron.
 
After the signal is delivered, the molecules drift back to the source neuron where they are collected and stored for future signaling.

Continue ›

 

The brain uses over 100 different neurotransmitters, but we only need to know about three: norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin.
 
The brain uses these for effort, enjoyment, and reward.


Norepinephrine  is   Effort
Dopamine  is   Enjoyment
Serotonin  is   Reward

Norepinephrine is effort

The first neurotransmitter of interest is norepinephrine, the neurotransmitter of effort.

You may have heard of epinephrine, also known as adrenaline (two words for the same substance). It's the chemical in EpiPens used to treat severe allergic reaction.

Epinephrine is released when you're afraid or angry, and causes your heart to race, your blood pressure to rise, and releases glucose into the blood for quick energy. It prepares your body (and mind) for a jolt of activity: fight or flight, something you might have to do to survive.

Nor-epinephrine, also known as nor-adrenaline (again: two words, same substance), is a weaker version used for day-to-day actions. Each time you decide to do (or not to do) something, your brain uses norepinephrine/noradrenaline.

If you've heard the expression "burning the candle at both ends", nor-epinephrine is like burning a candle, and epinephrine is like burning rocket fuel.

Continue ›

The effort threshold

When your brain decides to act, it releases norepinephrine and starts a counter to monitor the amount used for the task.
 
When the norepinephrine exceeds a threshold limit, the counter generates frustration, irritation, dissatisfaction, and annoyance to encourage the brain to stop.
 
Here are two tasks showing the counter limit.

The first task requires many actions. The total norepinephrine exceeds the limit, resulting in frustration and annoyance. The brain quits before reaching the goal.

The second task is less demanding. Using fewer and easier actions, the goal is reached before the threshold limit.

Dopamine is enjoyment

The second neurotransmitter (of three) is dopamine, the neurotransmitter of enjoyment.
 
Dompamine is released when you are "on the right path" making progress towards your goal. It counteracts norepinephrine, and gives a pleasant feeling of enjoyment.

 

A lengthy task might require many decisions and actions, but dopamine keeps the counter at bay until the goal is reached.
 
The journey is a pleasant experience.

Continue ›

 

Dopamine brings a pleasant feeling when you're making progress towards your goal.

Many recreational drugs, such as cocaine, work by increasing dopamine in the brain. Saying that dopamine is pleasant is perhaps an understatement.

It's possible to get so deeply involved with a project that the high levels of dopamine result in a tremendously pleasurable experience. A future lesson will have more on this.

For now, the takeaway is that the journey makes a project pleasurable, not the results.

Leaving things undone

One way to make starting easier is to leave something undone.

Writers sometimes do this. Instead of taking a break when a chapter is finished, they stop a half page before the end of a chapter, or after the first page of the next chapter.

When they sit down again, the next paragraphs are obvious, the dopamine kicks in, and they continue.

If you have trouble starting a project session, try leaving something undone in the previous session. Install all the bolts except one, don't sew the last hem, leave 1 component unplaced on the board layout, and so on.

When you return to your project, the undone task will help you restart.

Serotonin is reward

The third and final neurotransmitter is serotonin, which the brain uses for changes in predicted reward.

Serotonin increases your dopamine when something unexpectedly favorable happens, and decreases it when something unexpectedly bad happens.

(Note: This function exists in your brain, and serotonin exists in your brain, but assigning this function to serotonin is probably incorrect. There's currently no consensus on what serotonin does, so I'll use the term for now and correct it later.)

Continue ›

 

Studies show that a year after winning the lottery, people return to the level of happiness they had before winning.

Studies also show that after a tragic life event (cancer, losing a limb), people generally return their level of happiness from before the event.

There seems to be a baseline level of happiness people get from their daily lives: from a rewarding job, raising kids, having a life partner, or generally when they feel their life is "on the right track".

Life brings with it a default level of dopamine.

Continue ›

 

There is also an adjustment mechanism: the dopamine reward prediction error.

When you consider an action, the brain looks forward in time and predicts the reward for achieving that goal. You get a dopamine release for that predicted reward.

Once the goal is reached, the actual reward is compared to the predicted reward: if extra reward is seen, serotonin encourages more dopamine saying, in effect, "next time have a more positive outlook".

If less reward than predicted is seen, serotonin causes dopamine to be withheld, reducing future predictions.

This feedback teaches our brain to make accurate predictions.

Continue ›


 

Prediction error is differential: it measures perceived differences in value.

Sudden jumps will change the base dopamine level, but permanent rewards will become less noticeable over time.

A pay raise makes you happy because the extra reward is different from what you used to get, but the feeling soon fades. A year later the perceived value is the same as the day before, and will be the same a day later, and you find yourself looking for another pay raise.

Lottery winners feel joy from the increased dopamine. A year later, the perceived value is the same as the previous day, the same as predicted for the next day, and the dopamine kick has faded. They've returned to their original level of happiness.

This is called the Hedonic Treadmill.

Continue ›

 

A project begins with a lightbulb moment: you visualize an outcome with rewards, and this raises your dopamine.
 
You have startup motivation.

Continue ›

 

Suppose you put the project off for a day. The predicted value is the same as the previous day, and the differential is not as great. The dopamine boost begins to fade.

A few of days later, the day-by-day prediction is constant and the initial dopamine boost is gone.


 
If you try to work on the project, you find yourself in the beginning "low dopamine" section of the diagram.

Faded motivation

If you have high initial enthusiasm for a project but have to postpone working on it, such as waiting for parts to arrive, you may find that by the time the parts arrive you've lost interest.

If you have high initial enthusiasm for a project but cannot complete it in a couple of days, you may find that you've lost interest.

The project was initially interesting because of the perceived rewards, but unless you can complete the project before the feeling fades, you will have no motivations to sustain you to the end of the project.

Reward motives will fade in a few days, but intrinsic motivations can sustain you to the end of the project.

The Perceptual Filter

The Reticular Activating System (hereafter: RAS) is the brain's perceptual filter. It stands between your sensations and conscious mind, and decides what is important.

As you read this, you can feel your clothes against your skin and your weight against the chair. You see the room beyond the display, hear background noise, and feel the temperature of the air.

None of this makes it to your conscious mind because the RAS decides that it's not important.

...yet a bug crawling up your leg would grab your attention. The RAS sounds the alarm, saying: "check this out now!"

Continue ›

 

The RAS filters both sensations and perceptions. For example, chatting with your neighbor you hear:

Garden is doing well, had to have our elderly cat put down, son is joining the air force, we're selling his car, the house needs painting, getting an estimate tomorrow, our daughter is getting married...

Now suppose you are in the market for a car. You know roughly the type, model, age, and mileage you want, and you're planning to visit a dealership this weekend.

That same conversation:

Garden is doing well, had to have our elderly cat put down, son is joining the air force, we're selling his car, the house needs painting, getting an estimate tomorrow, our daughter is getting married...

You ask: Oh? What kind of car? How much are you asking?

You find that her son's car matches your needs, it's a good price, they're reliable sellers, so you make an offer.

Continue ›

 

In the first conversation, nothing stood out because your RAS decided none of it would help your goals.

In the second example the mention of a car stands out. The RAS alerts your conscious mind to the possibility of relevance, you inquired further, and found that it satisfies your goals.

The RAS is part of the brain's goal seeking mechanism.

Our daily lives are awash in possibilities, things we see, things we hear, things of benefit we could look into, that go unnoticed because the RAS decides they aren't relevant.

When we set clear goals, our RAS alerts us when possibilities arise, which leads to more chances at success.

Some people suggest this is how prayer works.

Goal Setting

There are many theories of Goal Setting, but in general goals should be:

  • Personal
  • Present
  • Positive
  • Measurable (if possible)
  • Time limited (if possible)

Personal

You can only set an RAS goal for yourself, not others. This may seem obvious, but it's surprising how many people try to set goals for their kids.
 
Use an "I" or "My" statement as a goal. Something like "I paint the hallway" or "I rebuild my engine".

Present

The RAS is an older part of the brain, evolutionarily speaking. It has an easier time when goals are expressed in the present tense.

Instead of "I will build a robot", use "I build a robot". Visualize the action in the present, instead of an indeterminate future date.

Continue ›


 

Positive

Keep your goals positive, in a logical sense. Set goals for what you will do, not what you won't.

The RAS, as an older part of the brain, has difficulty with reversed logic.

Measurable

If possible, express your goals in a measurable way.

Your brain perceives goal as a journey. Measurable progress makes the goal seem more journey-like, which is what your norepinephrine/dopamine system is evolved for.

Time Limited

If possible, set a reasonable end date, and track your progress over time to the end date.

This also makes the goal appear as a journey, which will better resonate with your RAS.

Project as Journey

Norepinephrine is the neurotransmitter that gets you going, and dopamine is released when you are on the right path.

Our ancestors needed food, shelter, or safety available at different places, and these neurotransmitters evolved to get us walking towards our goal.

We can strengthen the effect by framing our projects as a journey.

Continue ›

Think about your project and write down the major steps to complete it. Don't add too much detail, just five or six steps to describe each major milestone of your project.

Write the steps with circles and arrows, like a city subway map or train station diagram.

For example, a ceramics project might have: Knead clay, form vase, bisque fire, glaze, glaze fire, photograph, describe, list on etsy.

An electronics project might have: Design circuit, breadboard circuit, schematic capture, board layout, order boards and parts, assemble, final test, list on tindie.


 
Write the steps top to bottom and leave room at the top. The extra space is for later lessons.
 
Do this by hand and not, for example, by typing into a computer.
 
Writing out longhand will give you a stronger effect.

Use colored pens to make your diagram stand out if you like.

The Project Plan

Project plans are generally neglected in hobby projects.

A clear project plan will program your RAS to help achieve your goal, while a fuzzy and indistinct plan can leave it idle and undirected.

The three parts of a project plan are idea, sketch, and decision.

Idea!

Every project begins with an idea. You see a need, you wonder if something is possible, it "just pops into your head".

It's a wonderful idea, you see endless possibilities, it holds significant value and, frankly, it's just plain neat! Let's get started!

But you know how serotonin works: the sudden realization of future value is a differential signal, your dopamine is spiked, and you're psyched about the idea.

You know this initial enthusiasm will fade in a couple of days.

Do you really want another project?

Continue ›

 

The idea phase is when project planning is most valuable.

Instead of immediately starting your project, resolve to wait three days.

During this time, develop a project plan: feature list, how it should look, how it should operate, implementation details, and so on. Modify, add, delete, and refine.

No idea comes into the world fully formed, and making improvements early will save enormous amounts of time backtracking and redoing. Thinking things through will allow you to choose shorter and easier paths, so that the project uses less effort.

Do as much development as you can before you start the project.

The differential hit

The differential hit from serotonin is the source of many shelved projects. The problem comes from doing things in the wrong order.

The idea phase is not the time to begin work on the project.

It's also not the time to order parts, and more generally it's not the time to take action. Your spiked dopamine will only cause you to make emotional decisions.

The first step is to decide whether to do the project.

Continue ›

Sketch

When you first get an idea, wait 3 days. Use this time to more fully describe the project.

Carry a notebook with you and as you think of changes and improvements, jot them down. Cross out ideas that don't work, add notes and annotate existing thoughts, draw rough sketches, diagrams and flow charts.

Make it messy!

A pad and pen (or pencil) is better suited to this than a phone or laptop. Switching between sketch, diagram, and text, circling and drawing arrows is much easer with pen and paper.

Adding better features and dropping poor ones will add value, which will increase the dopamine boost.

And writing clear goals and visualizing the paths and outcomes will program your RAS to aim for the final goal.

Waiting 3 days and clarifying the project is a surprisingly effective technique.

Franklin's Method

Divide a piece of paper into two columns by drawing a line down the middle, and label the left column "pro" and the right column "con".

(This is the Ben Franklin Method for making a decision.)

Write the benefits of doing the project in the left column. You already have some of these in the motivation lesson, just copy over the intrinsic motivations and the extrinsic rewards.

 

Your first question should be: "Are there enough (any?) intrinsic motivations in the pro column?"

If not, then don't do the project. It will be tedious and boring.

Continue ›

 

In the right column, enter the costs the project will bring.

Estimate the calendar time you will need, your personal time across the calendar time, material costs, probability of failure, probability of injury (if any), and anything else that would argue against the project.

Include the lost opportunity costs as well. What are you giving up while working on the project? What would the money otherwise be spent on, what would you be doing with your time?

Don't forget the opportunity benefits. Would a new product drive extra sales to your web site? Would the added knowledge be valuable in your job? Help you get a promotion? Would the finished project save you future time?

List the reasons for and against the project in two columns.

As with everything, take a few days to add reasons as you think of them.

Continue ›

 

With the pros and cons laid out, now assign a gut-feel value to each. Connect items of similar value, and see what's left over.

You may feel that the knowledge gained will compensate for the materials cost, the time saved offsets the time spent, the ad revenue will be worth the effort.


 

There's no rule for measuring the importance of anything here, just whatever you feel are the right values for rewards and costs.

Eliminate pros and cons of roughly equal value, and see what's left over.

Write down a clear decision as a present, positive, and personal statement.


 

If you decide to do the project, then you can start working.

Ignoring Failure

Thomas Edison famously made over 10,000 failed attempts before he got a working light bulb.

When asked by a reporter how it felt to fail 10,000 times, Edison replied: “I didn't fail 10,000 times, I’ve just found 10,000 ways that will not work.”

This lesson isn't about motivation now, it's about keeping motivation when you encounter a failure.

Imagining future failures can minimize their impact. Also, thinking about failure can encourage taking steps to avoid it.

Note the word "when" and not "if" above. It's a near certainty that you'll encounter failure at some point, and getting properly oriented beforehand will help.

The term for this is "prospection".

Continue ›

 

So then, how should we handle failure?
 
First, note that failure comes at the end of a project, or perhaps causes the end. If you're still trying, if there's still a way it might work, then you haven't failed.
 
Next, in almost all cases failure is a stepping stone to success. There are innumerable examples of people failing repeatedly, only to succeed in the end. Thomas Edison is an extreme example (10,000 tries? Yikes!), but the principle is everywhere.
 
And finally, even when a project fails, is it a complete failure?
 
Consider:

  • Did you learn anything new?
  • Did you improve your technique?
  • Can you salvage something useful for another project?

It's extremely rare for a project to fail completely.
 
In nearly all cases there is value in having made the journey.

Flow States

A flow state is when you're doing something and you lose track of time. Many people experience flow states while driving: you're thinking about something, turning over a problem in your mind, and arrive at your destination with no memory of driving or of the passage of time.

Some people experience flow states in their projects. They sit down to work, get caught up in the activity, and look up hours later wondering where the time went.

Flow states are enjoyable, and the enjoyment builds the longer you are in the state. When you enter flow, it's as if your brain starts a counter, and the higher the count the more pleasure you get. When you stop working, the counter resets to zero, and if you restart your counter needs to build back up before you can get a similar level enjoyment.

The flow state counter will reset every time you stop working, so for maximum pleasure you can seek to avoid interruptions and breaks while in a flow state.

The Cognitive Gas Tank

One curious result from psychological studies is that cognition is a limited resource.

It's as if you wake up each morning with a gas tank full of cognitive ability, and throughout the day every action you take and every decision you make uses up a little bit of it.

During sleep, your gas tank fills up so that, hopefully, you wake up refreshed, with a full tank.

Continue ›

Studies show that it's possible to drain your cognition completely, and when that happens you lose the ability to make rational decisions.

This explains how you can come home at night and feel exhausted, even though you know you're not physically tired. Your cognitive gas tank is sitting at empty, and all you want to do is veg out in front of the T.V.

"Daddy's too tired to play with you tonight, pumpkin. Let's take a rain check."

Continue ›

It's OK to feel exhausted at the end of the day occasionally - we're probably evolved for that - but if you're feeling exhausted every evening then that should be fixed. Constant mental exhaustion is the same as constant mental stress (it uses the same neurotransmitters), and this causes incremental damage.

It causes depression, reduces your immune function, and leads to all sorts of problems later in life.

It's sort of like smoking. The damage from a single cigarette probably won't affect your health appreciably, but smoking continually over several years and the damage will add up.

Constant stress exhaustion is like that

Tuning your project

Your brain is a goal-seeking system.

Once you choose a destination, your brain mechanisms work to get you there, in opposition to other mechanisms that encourage you to stop.

We can increase our project enjoyment by tuning our projects to more strongly resonate with these mechanisms.

There's a tremendous payoff for doing this.

Continue ›

Eliminate pain

The first and most important way to increase your enjoyment is to eliminate pain.

All of it.

Pain feeds directly into the norepinephrine counter in your brain: it makes you want to stop whatever it is that you're doing.

Dopamine is literally an analgesic, which means it will mask the sensation of pain. You can burn yourself soldering, scrape a knuckle removing a stuck bolt, cut yourself with an x-axto knife... and never realize it. Often times, it's only hours later that you start to feel the pain, notice the injury, and wonder "when did I do this?"

Your dopamine caused you to ignore it.

Of course, if the pain then becomes more insistent it will reach a level that can overwhelm the dopamine effect and you are forced to stop.

Continue ›

 

As a general rule of thumb, you should be able to work on your project for 10 hours without pain.

If after a couple of hours you find that your joints are stiff, your neck is sore, your back is bothering you... adjust your work area.

The biggest offender here is back ache, which is caused by being bent over for long periods and fixed by raising your work area so that you aren't bent over.

Lathe operators typically hunch over their work, only to find a couple of days later that their back is bothering them, and are on disability a few weeks later.

Raise the lathe 4 inches and the back problems go away.

Priming

Priming is when a stimulus raises the brain's awareness of related concepts.

An example should make this clear. Read the following passage, then answer the question on the next page.

For my money, there was never a better shoe than the Anaheim sneaker. Whatever the surface or the size of the arena, any time a short-sided game crept up, you would find me wearing Anaheims. Mid-rise collar for stability and a natural fit for small feet, elastic laces and hook & loop closure made it easy to get on and off. Traction, cushioning, and the overall feel provided excellent grip on any surface.
 
The biggest issue for me was an odd fit/looseness in the heel. This happened every time I put the shoes on, but it went away after a few minutes of use.

Hint: It helps to read the passage aloud. See if you can discover It's odd nature.

Continue ›

 

Think of a 4-letter word beginning with S, O, L.

When you have one, type a letter to enter your word.

SOL

Probability of English words:
 
"SOLD": 0.00493391% (about once every  20200 words)
"SOLE": 0.00271912% (about once every  36700 words)
"SOLO": 0.00017536% (about once every 570200 words)

People typically guess "SOLD", since it is the most common.

Surprisingly, people who first read the shoe passage often answer "SOLE". The paragraph primes shoe related concepts in the brain, even though "SOLE" did not appear in the paragraph, even as a substring.

Priming activated the related concept.

You are now primed. Shoe related items are now emphasized in your brain, and this will affect your decisions and actions until the effect wears off.

Continue ›

 

Priming fades with time, but there is no consensus for how long it lasts. Some studies claim it decays over a few hours, but others have shown a small effect measurable a week after the original stimulus.

There are also different types of priming, and it is not known if the different types have different durations.

The slideshow uses priming to get your mind thinking about the project. We show images and words related to your project, and repeat this daily for a stronger effect.

The Wikipedia page on priming has more information.

Extrinsic and intrinsic motivation

Extrinsic motivations are what you get for completing a project, such as money, fame, encouragement, likes, subscribers, or avoiding punishment. They are the rewards you get in exchange for being done.

Intrinsic motivations come from the project itself, and there are four types:

  • Autonomy - You decide what to do
  • Growth - You learn something new, or practice something you're rusty at
  • Excellence - You do a good job
  • Usefulness - The project is valuable to you, your family, or the community

The Overjustification Effect

The overjustification effect is when your mind notices being rewarded for an activity, and assumes this is the reason you're doing it. Extrinsic motivations replace your intrinsic ones, and the intrinsic motivations fade away.
