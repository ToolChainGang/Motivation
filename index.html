Overview

Many people have difficulty bringing their projects to completion. They get excited at first, but the passion soon fades and the project languishes for lack of time.

Then you get a new idea that seems wonderful and exciting, but in a little while this new excitement also fades, and now you have two projects languishing for lack of time.

Rinse, and repeat.

Over time, many people amass multiple, sometimes dozens, of projects that they recognize as valuable, and would "really like" to get back to, but they never seem to have the time or motivation to do so.

Continue ›

 

This program uses psychological methods to increase your motivation for completing projects. Run it once a day for 30 days, and you should see an improvement in project motivation.

Each run consists of a one-minute slideshow of words and images that bring your project to mind, plus a short lesson on some aspect of motivation.

The slideshow gets your mind thinking about the project, and the lessons help you make small changes to your behavior and attitude that should boost your project motivation.

The techniques are well documented and backed by scientific studies, no tricks or subliminal messaging is used. All techniques are described in the "Methods" link on the main page.

Q: Does this work?
 
A: No one knows.

The premise of this program is that extrinsic goals have replaced your intrinsic motives, and your intrinsic motives have faded. That this happens is well established in the literature.

What isn't known, because it hasn't been studied, is whether this situation can be reversed. Hence this program.

It's 5 minutes of your time each morning for 30 days.

Try it, it's experimental, and if it doesn't work you're no worse off than you were at the start.

Extrinsic and intrinsic motivation

Extrinsic motivations are what you get for completing a project, such as money, fame, encouragement, likes, subscribers, or avoiding punishment. They are the rewards you get in exchange for being done.

Intrinsic motivations come from the project itself, and there are four types:

  • Autonomy - You decide what to do
  • Growth - You learn something new, or practice something you re rusty at
  • Excellence - You do a good job
  • Usefulness - The project is valuable to you, your family, or the community

Continue ›

 

Extrinsic motivations work well for routine, repetitive tasks - things you can do automatically, without a lot of thought or planning.
 
For example, a dealership auto mechanic spends his day diagnosing and fixing one brand of cars. The procedures are well known, require little thought or choice, and the paycheck is his extrinsic reward.

Intrinsic motivations work well for creative tasks.
 
If that same mechanic works at home on a project race car, he has intrinsic motivations:

  • He chooses what to do, and how to do it.
  • He learns about a different class of vehicle
  • He does a good job, and the car looks good
  • He wants to start racing

While working on his a project car, the mechanic gains a deep sense of satisfaction.
 
He enjoys the process.

Decreasing motivation

Extrinsic motivations will decrease the intrinsic motivations.

In one study, nursery school students were given markers and encouraged to draw pictures during their free play time, an activity for which they had high intrinsic interest.

Several weeks later, students in one group were given a “good player” award with a ribbon and gold star for drawing, while students in the control group were simply encouraged to draw for fun. Several weeks after that, both groups were monitored when the markers were brought out during playtime.

Surprisingly, children given an extrinsic reward showed far less interest in drawing than the children who were not offered the reward.

Something about the reward reduced the children’s desire to draw.

Continue ›

 

In another study, two groups of college students were asked to perform activities that they already found interesting: solving puzzles and writing newspaper headlines.

In the first phase of the study, both groups performed the activity with no rewards.

In the second phase, one group was paid for the activity, while the second group acted as a control.

In the third phase, both groups once again performed the activity with no rewards.

Students who were paid during the second phase were less likely to continue the activities in the third phase. Students who were not paid were more likely to continue.

Both groups started out equally interested, but the extrinsic reward reduced the motivation for one group.

Continue ›

The Overjustification Effect

This is called the overjustification effect.

Essentially, your mind notices being rewarded for an activity, and assumes this is the reason you're doing it. Extrinsic motivations replace your intrinsic ones, and the intrinsic motivations fade.

But then, the extrinsic rewards only come when the project is complete...

So with no intrinsic motivation to sustain you, making progress on your projects becomes a boring drudgery. You lose interest over time, and eventually give up.

If you have trouble finishing your projects, probably your intrinsic motivations have been replaced with extrinsic ones, leaving no motivation to sustain you while working on your project.

You'll need to fix that.

Writing out longhand will give you a stronger effect, but no one knows why this is so.

Actions can change Beliefs

Your actions can change your beliefs.

In one study researchers had two groups perform a boring task for an hour. Subjects from the first group were then asked to convince another person (an actor) that the task was interesting and engaging, while the second group acted as a control.

Curiously, subjects who tried to convince a third person that the task was actually interesting rated the tasks less boring than the subjects in the control group.

Their brains noticed a disconnect between their beliefs and their actions, and changed their beliefs to become consistent.

Continue ›

Concordant self-image

In another study, when participants were asked to give an impromptu speech supporting an opinion they disagreed with, their private opinion moved closer to the opinion in the speech.

Again, it seems that their brains noticed the disconnect between what they believed and how they acted, and changed beliefs to make a more consistent self image.

Your brain tries to maintain a concordant self-image. When your actions aren't consistent with your beliefs, your mind will change beliefs to match your actions.

You can use this effect to change your project motivations.

Taking action

Fold up the bottom of your motivation list so that it covers the extrinsic rewards. Cover the entire rewards section, so that you can't see any of it, and crease the fold with your fingernail.

The rewards are still there, you haven't deleted them, but right now you can't see them. Tell yourself you'll focus on the goals you can see.

Mantras

The concordant self-image technique uses actions to change your project beliefs.
 
Masking the rewards portion of your list will help change your beliefs, but this is only one action.


We can get a stronger effect by using more actions.
 
We do this by adding a mantra: a word or phrase recited during the day to reinforce the motivational beliefs.

Mantras get a lot of bad press, and probably for good reason. They are used for all sorts of vague and sketchy reasons, with a strong mystical component.
 
We won't be doing anything mystical, just adding an action to help your self-image become concordant. Concordant with a maker who enjoys the process.

Continue ›

Homework

Look over your motivation list and think of a mantra that will reinforce your self-image. Something that speaks to you, something you like.

Nothing long, just a couple of words. It can be generic, such as "I enjoy the process", or specific to your project such as "I love writing detective novels".

Use personal pronouns and put your verb in present tense. Starting with "I" is typical.
 
A mantra should also be positive, in the logical sense. don't say "I don't" or "I won't", brains have a harder time with inverted logic. Phrase it in terms of what you will do, instead of what you won't.
 
‹- Hint: For a stronger effect, write it down.

Motivational focus

Intrinsic motivations work well for creative tasks, and extrinsic motivations (rewards) work well for dull, repetitive tasks.

Some projects are a mixture of both.

For example, a car project might end with wet-sanding and painting. You might not find sanding and painting as interesting as the engine rebuild.

An electronics project might be followed by video editing and dubbing sessions for a YouTube channel.

And after creating a beautiful ceramic piece, a tedious session of photographing, writeup, pricing, and listing may be required.

Continue ›

 

If your project has a boring part at the end, you can change the endpoint of your project.

Place your motivation list on top of an empty sheet of paper, and fold the bottom up over the existing end point. Write in a new end point up to, but not including, the boring bits that come at the end.


As before, tape the flap shut and tell yourself you won't worry about those tasks.
 
Look over the list for a few moments to let that sink in.

Continue ›

 

You can also try switching motivations. This can work if a long, boring task comes in the middle. When you reach the boring task, switch from intrinsic motivations to extrinsic ones.

Promise yourself a reward for successful completion: go out to dinner, eat a bowl of ice cream, take a walk in the park - whatever you enjoy, use that as a reward for finishing the boring section.

Keep your task motivations separate. Any overlap between the rewards and the enjoyment will reduce your enjoyment.

Be clear with yourself that the rewards are for completing the task, and not for the entire project. Think about the rest of the project in terms of intrinsic enjoyment.

Restarting your project

At some point during this program you may feel the urge to get back into your project.

When that happens, go for it!

Don't wait until the end of this course to continue your project, you'll get a stronger effect if you're working on something while learning about motivation.

Take a moment to imagine yourself working on your project. Try and see yourself taking up where you left off, doing the next tasks, planning the next steps... whatever comes next in your project.

Visualize yourself doing this before the end of tomorrow night. See yourself today, or tonight, or tomorrow, or tomorrow night getting back to your project. Try to visualize this as clearly as you can, for several seconds.

The neurochemistry of motivation

Knowing the neurochemistry of motivation can help. Knowing how things work and how they interrelate will help you make informed decisions about your motivation.

The next few lessons describe, in broad terms, how the brain manages motivation using neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors.

The scientific literature for neurochemistry has changed since the author learned it 10 years ago. It's also wildly complex, and there's no consensus.

What follows is believed to be accurate for what happens and can be used for illustration and planning, but the names might be wrong.

Continue ›

Neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors

Neurotransmitters are chemicals that one neuron uses to signal another.

This happens at a special point called a synapse, where one neuron almost touches another. When the neuron wants to send a signal, it releases neurotransmitter molecules at the synapse, which diffuse to the other neuron and bind to corresponding receptors.


It's sort of like ringing a desk bell. Sending neurotransmitter molecules through the synapse alerts the target neuron.
 
After the signal is delivered, the molecules drift back to the source neuron where they are collected and stored for future signaling.

Continue ›

 

More than 100 neurotransmitters are used in the brain, but we only need to know about three: norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin.
 
The brain uses these for effort, enjoyment, and reward.


Norepinephrine  is   Effort
Dopamine  is   Enjoyment
Serotonin  is   Reward

Norepinephrine is effort

The first neurotransmitter of interest is norepinephrine, the neurotransmitter of effort.

You may have heard of epinephrine, also known as adrenaline (two words for the same substance). It's the chemical in EpiPens used to treat severe allergic reaction.

Epinephrine is released when you're afraid or angry, and causes your heart to race, your blood pressure to rise, and releases glucose into the blood for quick energy. Epinephrine/Adrenaline prepares your body (and mind) for a jolt of activity: fight or flight, something you might have to do to survive.

Nor-epinephrine, also known as nor-adrenaline (again: two words, same substance), is a weaker version used for day-to-day actions. Eact time you decide to do (or not to do) something, your brain uses norepinephrine/noradrenaline.

If you've heard the expression "burning the candle at both ends", nor-epinephrine is like burning a candle, and epinephrine is like burning rocket fuel.

Continue ›

The effort counter

When your brain decides to act, it releases norepinephrine and starts a counter that monitors the amount used for the task.
 
When the counter exceeds a threshold limit, it will generate frustration, irritation, dissatisfaction, and annoyance to encourage the brain to stop.

Here are two tasks showing the epinephrine counter.


 

The first task is complex, requiring many decisions. Eventually, the total norepinephrine exceeds the counter limit, resulting in frustration and annoyance. The brain tires of the task, and quits before reaching the goal.

The second task is less demanding. Requiring fewer and easier decisions, the goal is reached before the counter limit is reached.

Dopamine is enjoyment

The second neurotransmitter (of three) is dopamine, the neurotransmitter of enjoyment.
 
Dompamine is released when you are "on the right path" and making progress towards your goal. It counteracts the norepinephrine, and gives a pleasant feeling of enjoyment.

 

A lengthy task might require many decisions and actions, but dopamine keeps the counter at bay until the goal is reached.
 
The journey is a pleasant experience.

Continue ›

 

Dopamine brings a pleasant feeling when you're making progress towards your goal.

Many recreational drugs, such as cocaine, work by increasing dopamine in the brain so saying that dopamine is pleasant is perhaps an understatement.

It's possible to get so deeply involved with a project that the high levels of dopamine result in a tremendously pleasurable experience. You'll see more about this in a future lesson.

For now, the takeaway is that the journey makes a project pleasurable, not the results.

Leaving things undone

One way to make starting easier is to leave something undone.

Writers sometimes do this. Instead of taking a break when a chapter is finished, they stop a half page before the end of a chapter, or after the first page of the next chapter.

When they sit down again, the next paragraphs are obvious, the dopamine kicks in, and they continue.

If you have trouble starting on your project, try leaving something undone in the previous session. Install all the bolts except one, don't sew the last hem, leave 1 component unplaced on the board layout, and so on.

When you return to your project, the undone task will help get you started again.

Serotonin is reward

The third neurotransmitter (of three) is serotonin, which the brain uses for changes in predicted reward.

If a distant uncle suddenly passes leaving you his fortune, your brain will release serotonin to cause a release of dopamine, which will make you happy.

If your elderly uncle is terminal, in hospice, and it's only a matter of time - in that case his passing is solemn and his legacy is received with somber respect.

Serotonin increases your dopamine when something unexpectedly favorable happens, and decreases it when something unexpectedly bad happens.

Continue ›

 

The full term for the action of serotonin is "dopamine reward prediction error".

When you consider an action, the brain looks forward in time and predicts the reward for achieving that goal. Serotonin causes a dopamine release for that predicted reward.

Once the goal is reached, the actual reward is compared to the predicted reward: if extra reward is seen, serotonin releases more dopamine saying, in effect, "next time have a more positive outlook".

If less reward than predicted is seen, serotonin causes dopamine to be withheld, reducing future predictions.

Serotonin is the feedback signal that teaches us to make accurate predictions.

Continue ›


 

Serotonin is a differential signal: it responds to a perceived difference in value.

A pay raise will make you happy for a time because the extra reward is different from what you used to get, but the feeling soon fades. A year later the perceived value is the same as the day before, and will be the same a day later, and now you're looking for another pay raise.

Studies show that a year after winning the lottery, most people return to the level of happiness they had before winning.

Winning spikes their serotonin, which increases their dopamine, and results in feelings of joy. A year later, the perceived value is the same as it was the previous day, the same as predicted for the next day, and no serotonin is released. The dopamine from the win has faded, and they've returned to their original level of happiness.

This is called the Hedonic Treadmill.

Happiness is always over the cognitive horizon.

Continue ›


 

 
A project typically starts with a lightbulb moment: you visualize an outcome, and the goal has value.
 
This releases serotonin, which raises your dopamine.
 
You're at the beginning, but motivated to do the project.

Suppose you put the project off for a day. Now the predicted value is the same as the previous day, the differential is not as great, and the serotonin (and corresponding dopamine boost) begins to fade.


 
A few of days later the day-by-day predicted value is constant, and the initial boost from serotonin is gone.
 
If you try to start the project, you find yourself in the initial "low dopamine" section of the diagram.

Faded motivation

If you start a project and have to order parts online, when the parts arrive you may find you've lost interest.

This is the root cause of the "startup" issue mentioned in the previous lesson, and the source of many paused projects.

One way to deal with this is to always wait a couple of days before starting a new project.

Ignoring Failure

Thomas Edison made over 10,000 failed attempts before he got a working light bulb.

When asked by a reporter how it felt to fail 10,000 times, Edison replied: “I didn't fail 10,000 times, I’ve just found 10,000 ways that will not work.”

This lesson isn't about motivation now, it's about keeping motivation when you encounter a failure.

Imagining future failures can minimize their impact. Also, thinking about failure can encourage taking steps to avoid it.

Note the word "when" and not "if" above. It's a near certainty that you'll encounter failure at some point, and getting properly oriented beforehand will help.

The term for this is "prospection".

Continue ›

So then, how should we handle failure?

First, note that failure comes at the end of a project, or perhaps causes the end. If you're still trying, if there's still a way it might work, then you haven't failed.

Next, in almost all cases failure is a stepping stone to success. There are innumerable examples of people failing repeatedly, only to succeed in the end. Thomas Edison is an extreme example (10,000 tries? Yikes!), but the principle happens everywhere.

And finally, even when a project fails, is it a complete failure?

Consider:

  • Did you learn anything new?
  • Did you improve your technique?
  • Can you salvage something useful for another project?

It's extremely rare for a project to fail completely. In nearly all cases there is still value in having made the journey.

Flow States

A flow state is when you're doing something and you lose track of time. Many people experience flow states while driving: you're thinking about something, turning over a problem in your mind, and arrive at your destination with no memory of driving or of the passage of time.

Some people experience flow states in their projects. They sit down to work, get caught up in the activity, and look up hours later wondering where the time went.

Flow states are enjoyable, and the enjoyment builds the longer you are in the state. When you enter flow, it's as if your brain starts a counter, and the higher the count the more pleasure you get. When you stop working, the counter resets to zero, and if you restart your counter needs to build back up before you can get a similar level enjoyment.

The flow state counter will reset every time you stop working, so for maximum pleasure you can seek to avoid interruptions and breaks while in a flow state.

The Cognitive Gas Tank

One curious result from psychological studies is that cognition is a limited resource.

It's as if you wake up each morning with a gas tank full of cognitive ability, and throughout the day every action you take and every decision you make uses up a little bit of it.

During sleep, your gas tank fills up so that, hopefully, you wake up refreshed, with a full tank.

Continue ›

Studies show that it's possible to drain your cognition completely, and when that happens you lose the ability to make rational decisions.

This explains how you can come home at night and feel exhausted, even though you know you're not physically tired. Your cognitive gas tank is sitting at empty, and all you want to do is veg out in front of the T.V.

"Daddy's too tired to play with you tonight, pumpkin. Let's take a rain check."

Continue ›

It's OK to feel exhausted at the end of the day occasionally - we're probably evolved for that - but if you're feeling exhausted every evening then that should be fixed. Constant mental exhaustion is the same as constant mental stress (it uses the same neurotransmitters), and this causes incremental damage.

It causes depression, reduces your immune function, and leads to all sorts of problems later in life.

It's sort of like smoking. The damage from a single cigarette probably won't affect your health appreciably, but smoking continually over several years and the damage will add up.

Project as Journey

Norepinephrine is the neurotransmitter that gets you going, and dopamine is released when you are on the right path. The word "path" is appropriate because the NE circuit evolved to get us walking towards our goal.

Our ancestors needed food, shelter, or safety which were found at different places, and the neurotransmitters act to get us there.

Since this is the mechanism that makes working on projects enjoyable, we can strengthen the effect by framing our projects as a journey.

We can link our projects more strongly to this mechanism by describing our project as a journey.

Priming

Priming is when a stimulus raises the brain's awareness of related concepts.

An example should make this clear. Read the following passage, then answer the question on the next page.

For my money, there was never a better shoe than the Anaheim sneaker. Whatever the surface or the size of the arena, any time a short-sided game crept up, you would find me wearing Anaheims. Mid-rise collar for stability and a natural fit for small feet, elastic laces and hook & loop closure made it easy to get on and off. Traction, cushioning, and the overall feel provided excellent grip on any surface.
 
The biggest issue for me was an odd fit/looseness in the heel. This happened every time I put the shoes on, but it went away after a few minutes of use.

Hint: It helps to read the passage aloud. See if you can discover It's odd nature.

Probability of English words:
 
"SOLD": 0.00493391% (about once every  20200 words)
"SOLE": 0.00271912% (about once every  36700 words)
"SOLO": 0.00017536% (about once every 570200 words)

People typically guess "SOLD", since it is the most common.

Surprisingly, people who first read the shoe passage often answer "SOLE". The paragraph primes shoe related concepts in the brain, even though "SOLE" did not appear in the paragraph, even as a substring.

Priming activated the related concept.

You are now primed. Shoe related items are now emphasized in your brain, and this will affect your decisions and actions until the effect wears off.

Priming fades with time, but there is no consensus for how long it lasts. Some studies claim it decays over a few hours, but others have shown a small effect measurable a week after the original stimulus.

There are also different types of priming, and it is not known if the different types have different durations.

The slideshow uses priming to get your mind thinking about the project. We show images and words related to your project, and repeat this daily for a stronger effect.

The Wikipedia page on priming has more information.

The Standout Word

The slideshow uses priming to get you thinking about your projects, but of course this won't work if you're not paying attention.

The "standout word" is just a mechanism to keep you focused. it's not counted or scored.

The type of highlighting is randomly determined - the idea is that if you don't know which word will be highlighted, you'll pay attention. Similarly, the number of highlights and type of question can vary.

The program presents a mix of highlights and options so that viewing the slideshow doesn't become automatic.

Extrinsic and intrinsic motivation

Extrinsic motivations are what you get for completing a project, such as money, fame, encouragement, likes, subscribers, or avoiding punishment. They are the rewards you get in exchange for being done.

Intrinsic motivations come from the project itself, and there are four types:

  • Autonomy - You decide what to do
  • Growth - You learn something new, or practice something you're rusty at
  • Excellence - You do a good job
  • Usefulness - The project is valuable to you, your family, or the community

The Overjustification Effect

The overjustification effect is when your mind notices being rewarded for an activity, and assumes this is the reason you're doing it. Extrinsic motivations replace your intrinsic ones, and the intrinsic motivations fade away.

Concordant self-image

Your brain maintains a concordant self-image.

When your brain notices a discrepancy between your beliefs and your actions, it will change beliefs to become consistent.

Prospection

Prospection is looking forward to possible futures.

The feelings thus evoked can cause you to anticipate whether future events are desirable or undesirable, and take appropriate actions in the present.

The course uses prospection to prepare your mind to handle possible future failures. Imagining that future softens the impact, and can lead to taking steps in the present to prevent it.
